Number Theory: Triangular Numbers

Solving the Handshake Problem

While working through some fun coding problems, I started to notice a few mathematical concepts that were unfamiliar to me. Naturally, I thought it would be fun to take a closer look at them to become more comfortable implementing them in my code. One concept was related to number theory, specifically triangular numbers. The classic problem used to demonstrate the concept was the handshake problem.

The handshake problem asks this: if n people walk into a room, and each person shakes hands once with everyone in the room, how many handshakes will there be?

As it turns out, thereâ€™s a simple equation to solve for this, and the resulting number will be a triangular number. The total number of handshakes will be equal to n * (n + 1) / 2 . So how can we make sense of this equation and start to understand the patterns behind it? Why, by looking at an infographic of course.

The first 36 triangular numbers are: 1, 3, 6, 10, 15, 21, 28, 36, 45, 55, 66, 78, 91, 105, 120, 136, 153, 171, 190, 210, 231, 253, 276, 300, 325, 351, 378, 406, 435, 465, 496, 528, 561, 595, 630, 666...

As shown above with dots, a group of just 4 people would have a total of 10 handshakes. You'll also notice that the number of people equals the number of dots on one side of the triangle. The equilateral triangular shape that the dots form are the reason why these numbers have their name.

The handshake problem is simple enough to code up in Ruby, I've defined the method handshake below to take in the number of people as an argument and return the number of handshakes.

										
def handshake(n)
  n*(n-1)/n
end
